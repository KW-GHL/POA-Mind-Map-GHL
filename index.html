Power of Attorney
Donee (Attorney): Duties and Limits
Lawyer: Independent Safeguard in the Process
May manage the donor’s finances and legal affairs only within the authority granted in the Power of Attorney document – e.g. operating bank accounts, signing contracts, buying or selling property
Must act in the donor’s best interests at all times and keep decisions consistent with any written instructions
Has a fiduciary duty to keep accurate records of all transactions; failure to do so is an offence
Cannot mix personal funds with the donor’s assets or benefit personally while acting
Authority ends automatically on the donor’s death, revocation or court intervention
May be personally liable for losses caused by negligence or misuse; serious misconduct can attract criminal penalties
No government body monitors the donee day-to-day; relatives can apply to SACAT or the Supreme Court if misconduct is suspected
Confirms legal capacity and free will before the donor signs; capacity doubt can invalidate the document later
Acts as an authorised witness: South Australian law requires the donor’s signature to be witnessed by a solicitor, JP or other authorised person for the instrument to be valid
Drafts clear, tailored wording so the scope, start date and any conditions (e.g. joint action, activation on medical certificate) are legally enforceable and understood
Advises whether the Power of Attorney must be lodged with Land Services SA before real-estate dealings and attends to lodging requirements
Provides certified copies accepted by banks, share registries and government agencies; stores the original securely for future reliance
Assists with future variations or revocation, ensuring all parties and institutions are properly notified to prevent outdated authority being used
Keeps contemporaneous file notes, providing independent evidence if the document or the donee’s actions are ever challenged
